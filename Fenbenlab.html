What is the action of fenbendazole?

Fenbendazole was originally designed to treat parasites by specifically inhibiting microtubule formation through attaching to -tubulin. This hinders the polymerization of tubulin dimers in parasite cells, causing them to die.

Surprisingly, when tested against tumor cells, fenbendazole and other benzimidazoles seem to have a similar impact. At the moment, it is thought that fenbendazole eliminates cancer cells in three ways:

1) Induction of apoptosis. This anti-tumor action is assumed to be achieved by the medicine's partnership with -tubulin, which results in the cell cycle being stopped as well as being cytotoxic.
The ability of benzimidazole carbamates to inhibit tubulin polymerization into microtubules in helminths and human tumor cells has been extensively established.

2) Inhibition of glucose use in cancer cells. Cancer cells are well-known for their high glucose absorption. Because of aerobic glycolysis, cancer cells often utilize glucose two hundred times faster than normal cells (the Warburg effect). This is evident in PET scans - the metabolically functioning regions that require increased radioactive glucose are plainly visible and are often thought to represent inflammation or malignant tumors.

3) The p53 gene's resurgence. This work process is still somewhat contentious, and further research is needed to demonstrate that fenbendazole causes this activity.
Nonetheless, an increasing quantity of studies has revealed that fenbendazole may actually stimulate the greatest tumor suppressor in the human body - p53.

4) Cancer cells do not develop chemo-resistance to fenbendazole. What's remarkable is that cancer cells may avoid this de-wormer medicine and adapt to its presence. Unfortunately, this happens with a large number of chemotherapy and biological treatment medications.

As a result, the tumors do not develop any resistance to fenbendazole; it remains effective and will continue to eliminate cancer cells, unlike paclitaxel, vinblastine, docetaxel, vincristine, or other types of chemotherapies.

5) Fenbendazole may make malignant tumors more sensitive to radiation. It may sensitize malignant tumors to radiation in a similar manner as taxane-based chemotherapeutic treatments.

Fenbendazole is a moderate microtubule disrupting drug that causes cancer cells to die.
